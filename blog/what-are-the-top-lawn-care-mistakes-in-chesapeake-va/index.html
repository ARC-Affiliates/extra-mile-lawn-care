What Is the Most Common Lawn Mowing Mistake in Chesapeake, VA?

The most common lawn mowing mistake in Chesapeake is cutting grass too short — usually below 2.5 inches.

Scalping the lawn weakens roots, exposes soil to weed seeds, and burns turf during Chesapeake's hot summers.

According to Extra Mile Lawn Care, tall fescue and turf-type fescue blends — the most popular cool-season grasses in Chesapeake neighborhoods like Greenbrier, Western Branch, and Great Bridge — should be mowed at 3 to 3.5 inches. Warm-season Bermuda and zoysia tolerate shorter cuts of 1.5-2 inches, but only with proper irrigation. The Virginia Cooperative Extension confirms taller blades shade the soil, reduce evaporation, and suppress crabgrass naturally (source: ext.vt.edu). Homeowners near the Chesapeake Square corridor often mow weekly with the deck set too low, which is why Extra Mile Lawn Care raises mower heights between June and September to protect roots from the I-64 corridor's reflected heat.

How Often Should You Water a Lawn in Chesapeake, VA?

Watering a Chesapeake lawn correctly means deep, infrequent irrigation — typically 1 inch of water spread across 1 or 2 sessions per week.

Daily light watering creates shallow root systems and increases fungal disease risk in Chesapeake's humid coastal air.

Experts at Extra Mile Lawn Care recommend watering between 4 AM and 9 AM so blades dry before the afternoon humidity peaks. Hampton Roads averages 46 inches of rain annually, so July and August are usually the only months requiring supplemental irrigation. Use a tuna can on the lawn to measure output — when it holds 1 inch, you've watered enough. Lawns in Hickory and Deep Creek, where sandy loam drains faster, may need slightly more. Avoid evening watering: standing moisture overnight encourages brown patch (a fungal disease — Rhizoctonia solani) that thrives in Chesapeake's August dew points above 72°F.

Why Is Fall Aeration So Important in Chesapeake?

Fall aeration relieves soil compaction caused by Chesapeake's heavy clay subsoils, allowing oxygen, water, and nutrients to reach grass roots before winter dormancy.

Core aeration in September or October delivers 3-4x more new root growth than spring aeration for cool-season lawns.

According to Extra Mile Lawn Care, lawns in older Chesapeake subdivisions — Indian River, Camelot, and parts of Western Branch — sit on compacted clay deposited during 1960s-1980s construction. Core aeration (a process that pulls 2-3 inch soil plugs from the lawn to relieve compaction) is the single highest-ROI service for these properties. Pair it with overseeding using a turf-type tall fescue blend rated for Virginia Zone 8a, and the lawn typically shows 30-50% improvement in density by the following May.

What Are Common Lawn Care Myths in Chesapeake?

Myth: Watering every day keeps grass healthier in summer.

Fact: Daily watering creates shallow roots; deep watering 1-2x weekly is healthier.

Myth: Spring is the best time to fertilize fescue.

Fact: September and November feedings deliver 3x the root growth of spring applications.

Myth: Bagging clippings produces a cleaner lawn.

Fact: Mulching clippings returns up to 25% of annual nitrogen needs back to the soil.
